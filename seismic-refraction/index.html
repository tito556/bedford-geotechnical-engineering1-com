The ground beneath Bedford changes character sharply from the gravel terraces of the Ouse valley to the Oxford Clay that underlies much of the town. A warehouse foundation near the river at Priory Marina encounters dense flint gravels at three metres, while a site upslope toward Brickhill sits on stiff, desiccated clay overlying the Great Oolite limestone. Seismic tomography resolves these transitions without a single borehole. By measuring P-wave and S-wave velocities across a geophone array, the method maps the bedrock profile, identifies fractured zones, and provides the shear-wave velocity data that Eurocode 7 demands for seismic site classification. In a town where the geology shifts within the span of a single postcode, seismic refraction profiling combined with reflection processing delivers a continuous subsurface image that isolated trial pits cannot match.

Seismic tomography turns a 2D velocity cross-section into a geotechnical section: rippable, rock-head, shear-wave velocity, all readable in one profile.

Methodology applied in Bedford

On a recent job off Barkers Lane, the client assumed limestone at three metres because a neighbour's extension found rock early. Our seismic refraction line showed otherwise: the bedrock dropped sharply into a solution hollow filled with soft clay, invisible from surface inspection. That is the kind of surprise Bedford's karstic limestone reliably produces. We run 24-channel or 48-channel spreads depending on target depth, using a sledgehammer source for shallow work and a weight drop when penetration beyond 30 metres is needed. Tomographic inversion, rather than simple layer-cake interpretation, handles lateral velocity gradients properly. The resulting 2D sections feed directly into rippability assessments, slope stability back-analyses, and foundation design. Every line is processed to BS 5930:2015+A1:2020 standards, with first-break picking verified by a second analyst before inversion. Where ground truth is available from boreholes, we tie the velocity model to logged strata, calibrating the geophysical interpretation against physical samples.

Risks and considerations in Bedford

The most frequent mistake on Bedford sites is treating seismic refraction as a standalone yes/no rock-head indicator, then stopping. A single refraction line gives apparent velocities; without tomographic inversion and without tying to at least one control point, a low-velocity zone caused by fracturing or a buried channel gets interpreted as competent rock, or vice versa. On one Castle Road project, a contractor relied on a basic refraction pick that missed a three-metre-wide dissolution feature, later exposed during excavation at significant programme cost. The fix is straightforward: run overlapping or orthogonal lines, process with curved-ray tomography, and calibrate against even a single dynamic probe or window sample. Where shear modulus is critical for foundation design, we pair the seismic survey with MASW testing to extract a continuous Vs profile, eliminating the guesswork from site classification to BS EN 1998-1.

Frequently asked questions

What depth can seismic tomography reach on a typical Bedford site?

With a sledgehammer source and 48-channel spread at 3-metre geophone spacing, we reliably image to 25–30 metres in the Oxford Clay and limestone. A weight-drop source extends the range to 50–60 metres where site access permits. The limiting factor in Bedford is often the water table in the river gravels, which improves energy coupling and actually helps penetration.

How much does a seismic tomography survey cost in Bedford?

A single refraction line with tomographic processing typically falls between £2,410 and £4,600, depending on the number of spreads, source type, and whether reflection acquisition is added. A site-specific quote is always provided after we review the proposed layout and target depth.

Can seismic methods detect dissolution features in the limestone?

Yes, provided the survey design is appropriate. A single refraction line may miss narrow features, so we recommend orthogonal lines or a small 3D grid where karst is suspected. Tomographic inversion highlights low-velocity anomalies that correspond to clay-filled hollows, and reflection processing can image the cavity roof where the impedance contrast is sufficient.

Do I still need boreholes if I have a seismic survey?

Seismic tomography reduces the number of boreholes needed and positions them intelligently, but it does not eliminate them entirely. At least one point of ground truth, typically a dynamic probe or window sample, is required to calibrate the velocity model to actual lithology. The combination of geophysics and targeted intrusive investigation gives the most reliable ground model.
