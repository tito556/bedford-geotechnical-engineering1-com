Along the river terraces and into the clay-rich uplands north of Bedford, the contrast in subsurface materials can be surprisingly sharp. In our experience, the Oxford Clay and the overlying river gravels along the Great Ouse present a classic target for electrical methods. A borehole tells you exactly what is at one point. A test pit offers a visual slice, but neither captures the lateral continuity of a gravel lens or the undulating bedrock surface across a 200-metre transect. That is where vertical electrical sounding earns its place. We run direct current through the ground and measure apparent resistivity at increasing electrode spacings, building a layered model of the subsurface without disturbing the site. For a town expanding into former brickwork clay pits and floodplain margins, knowing where the competent limestone begins, or where a perched water table sits within the terrace deposits, shapes everything from foundation depth to dewatering design.

Resistivity data gives you the electrical stratigraphy. Combined with a borehole log, it turns a single point into a continuous underground section.

Methodology applied in Bedford

The geology between the Brickhill area and the town centre is not uniform, and the resistivity response reflects that. On the higher ground towards Manton Heights, the Great Oolite limestone can present as a high-resistivity horizon above 150 ohm-m, often masking deeper clay layers until we run a longer Schlumberger spread. Down by the Embankment, the alluvial silts and gravels create a multi-layer profile: a low-resistivity cap of saturated silt, a mid-range gravel unit, and then a sharp drop into the Oxford Clay. We adjust electrode geometry for each context. A Wenner array works well for shallow gravel mapping, but we default to Schlumberger when the target is deeper than 15 metres. For brownfield sites where relict foundations or buried services create noise, we often pair resistivity with MASW to cross-check stiffness profiles against the electrical stratigraphy—shear wave velocity does not lie, even when a buried pipe distorts the resistivity image.

Risks and considerations in Bedford

Bedford's growth through the 19th and 20th centuries was fuelled by brickmaking, and many of the clay pits that fed the kilns were later backfilled with whatever material was at hand. We have surveyed sites near the old Stewartby and Elstow works where the fill is a chaotic mix of overburden, brick waste, and organic silt. Electrical resistivity cuts through that confusion. A low-resistivity zone that does not align with any natural geology is often the signature of saturated fill or a buried channel. Missing it means differential settlement under a raft foundation or unexpected groundwater inflow during basement construction. The risk is higher on the floodplain where a thin gravel aquifer sits directly over clay: if the gravel is thinner than the borehole suggests, dewatering volumes are underestimated. VES reduces that uncertainty by mapping the gravel base continuously between boreholes, giving the ground model the lateral detail a single log cannot provide.

Our services

Our resistivity services in Bedford cover the full workflow from field acquisition to interpreted ground model:

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

Schlumberger array soundings for depth-to-bedrock, clay thickness mapping, and groundwater exploration. We process each sounding through inversion software to produce a layered resistivity model tied to available borehole control.

Combined geophysical-ground investigation

Resistivity traverses integrated with dynamic probing or cable percussion boreholes. We use the resistivity cross-section to select optimal borehole locations, reducing the total number of intrusive points while increasing model coverage.

Frequently asked questions

What does a VES survey in Bedford typically cost?

For a standard vertical electrical sounding with a Schlumberger array reaching 40–50 metres depth, budget between £570 and £820. The exact figure depends on access conditions, the number of soundings, and whether we need to combine it with MASW or a short resistivity traverse for lateral control.

How deep can electrical resistivity soundings reach in the Bedford area?

With a maximum current electrode separation of 200 metres, we can investigate to roughly 60–80 metres depth in favourable conditions. On the Oxford Clay, the practical limit is often shallower because the low-resistivity clay attenuates the signal. For deeper targets, we often recommend combining VES with seismic refraction to get both electrical and mechanical boundaries.

What is the difference between a VES sounding and a resistivity imaging profile?

A VES sounding uses a single expanding array centred on one point to build a 1D layered model directly beneath that point. A resistivity imaging profile uses multiple electrodes along a line to produce a 2D cross-section. We use VES when the geology is roughly horizontal and we need depth control at a specific location. We recommend 2D imaging when the target is laterally discontinuous, such as a buried channel or a fault zone.
