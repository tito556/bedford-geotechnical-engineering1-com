In Bedford, the contrast between the gravel terraces along the Great Ouse and the stiff Oxford Clay beneath means one compaction target never fits all jobs. We see it regularly on A6 corridor projects: a contractor uses a single moisture-density spec, then hits trouble where the river deposits shift. Proctor testing removes that guesswork. Our lab runs both Standard and Modified Proctor to BS 1377-4:1990, pinning down the exact optimum moisture content and maximum dry density for the material on your site. For projects involving deep excavations in the town centre, the soft ground approach often relies on the fill quality determined by these tests. The data feeds directly into the Method Specification or End-Product Specification you submit to the local authority.

A single Proctor curve replaces a dozen assumptions about how local soil will behave under a roller.

Methodology applied in Bedford

Bedford sits at roughly 40 m above Ordnance Datum, but the ground conditions vary sharply within half a mile. The River Great Ouse has left pockets of soft alluvium, while the Woburn Sands formation appears just east of town. That geology means a Modified Proctor (4.5 kg rammer, 457 mm drop) often applies to granular sub-base near the A421 bypass, whereas Standard Proctor (2.5 kg rammer, 300 mm drop) suits the clay fill behind a retaining structure in Brickhill. Our procedure follows BS 1377-4 tightly: we screen material through a 20 mm or 37.5 mm sieve, run five points to build the full compaction curve, and report air voids percentage. The result is a defensible target density that your roller operator can chase with confidence. We also cross-check with sand replacement density on site when the spec demands it.

Risks and considerations in Bedford

A pavement failure on a Bedford retail park a few years ago traced back to one thing: the structural fill under the car park was placed 4 % below optimum moisture. No Proctor curve had been run on the borrowed Oxford Clay, so the contractor aimed for a density that was physically unreachable with the available plant. The result was long-term settlement and cracking that cost far more to remedy than the test programme would have. We also see risk where imported granular fill from the Greensand Ridge is assumed to behave like Type 1 sub-base; a Modified Proctor on the actual material often reveals a different OMC. In floodplain zones near Priory Country Park, post-compaction saturation can collapse the air voids structure if the density target was never founded on a proper laboratory compaction curve.

Our services

The Proctor test anchors every compaction spec we write, but a full earthworks package often needs more. These are the two services our Bedford clients combine most frequently with the Proctor programme.

In-Situ Density by Sand Replacement

Using the sand cone method to BS 1377-9, we verify that the placed fill in your Bedford site reaches the relative compaction percentage you committed to. The lab Proctor gives the target; the field density test confirms you hit it.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a Proctor test cost for a Bedford soil sample?

For a single point on the compaction curve we charge between £80 and £120. A full five-point Standard or Modified Proctor with the curve, optimum moisture content, and maximum dry density report typically ranges from £120 to £180, depending on the material fraction and whether we need to run a separate particle density determination.

Should I specify Standard or Modified Proctor for a Bedford housing estate road?

For residential estate roads adopted under Section 38, the local highway authority usually references MCHW Series 600, which defaults to the Modified Proctor effort (4.5 kg rammer). Standard Proctor is rarely used for pavement sub-base in the UK anymore. For landscape areas or low-loading fill behind retaining walls, Standard Proctor can be appropriate and more achievable with lighter compaction plant. We can advise once we see the material and the specification.

How long does it take to get Proctor results?

A standard turnaround is three to four working days from sample delivery to our soil laboratory. We can process a rush Proctor in 24 hours if the material dries quickly in the oven. The limiting step is always the moisture content determinations at each point, which cannot be accelerated without compromising the result.

What sample size do you need for a Proctor test?

We need roughly 25 kg of disturbed material for a 1-litre mould test or 40 kg for the 2.3-litre mould. The sample should be bagged and sealed immediately after excavation to preserve the natural moisture content. If the material contains particles larger than 37.5 mm, we will discuss whether a replacement technique or a scalping procedure is appropriate under BS 1377-4.
