The Great Ouse cuts through Bedford on a bed of Jurassic Oxford Clay, capped in places by Quaternary river gravels. Site class can shift sharply in under 100 metres here. A VS30 profile derived from Multichannel Analysis of Surface Waves gives you the shear wave velocity to 30 metres depth—the parameter Eurocode 8 uses to define ground type for seismic design. We run MASW arrays on brownfield plots near the Embankment, on chalky boulder clay outcrops north of town, and on the gravel terraces where stiffness drops fast with depth. The data feeds directly into EC8 site classification and foundation response spectra. When the near-surface geology is this variable, relying on a desk-study VS30 proxy can misclassify the whole site and drive up foundation costs unnecessarily.

A 24-channel array resolves the Oxford Clay–river gravel contact within half a metre, turning a generic ground type into a measured VS30.

Methodology applied in Bedford

Bedford’s Victorian expansion pushed terraced housing onto the river gravels, while post-war estates moved onto the boulder clay plateau. Each formation carries a different stiffness signature and a different site period. Our MASW acquisition uses a 24-channel seismograph with 4.5 Hz geophones at 2-metre spacing—tight enough to resolve velocity inversions that a wider spread would miss. We process the dispersion image with a combination of phase-shift and frequency-wavenumber transforms, picking the fundamental-mode curve where energy is strongest. The inversion runs iteratively against a layered half-space model until the misfit between calculated and observed dispersion drops below 2%. For sites where the clay is overlain by made ground, we sometimes cross-check the profile with a seismic refraction line to pin down the fill thickness independently. On larger commercial schemes we also run a CPT to calibrate the velocity-sleeve-friction correlation and tighten the ground model for pile design.

Risks and considerations in Bedford

Eurocode 8 Part 1 tabulates five ground types from A to E, each with a different elastic response spectrum. Misclassifying a Bedford site by even one class—say, calling stiff Oxford Clay ‘Type B’ when a thin buried soft layer drags VS30 below 360 m/s—can double the design base shear and force a re-design of the lateral-load-resisting system. The risk is highest on the floodplain, where Holocene alluvium sits directly on the clay and creates a velocity reversal that borehole logs alone rarely detect. MASW captures that reversal as a drop in phase velocity at low frequencies, flagging it before the foundation loads are finalised. On sites where the profile suggests borderline Type C/D conditions, we recommend deepening the investigation with a single downhole shot to confirm the 30-metre average directly, removing the last ambiguity from the seismic design parameters.

Our services

MASW is rarely the only geophysical tool on a Bedford site. We package it with complementary methods so the ground model is internally consistent.

VS30 Site Classification

Full dispersion processing, inversion, and VS30 calculation per EN 1998-1. Deliverables include the dispersion image, 1D shear-wave velocity profile, and the EC8 ground-type letter. We supply the raw SEG-2 files on request for third-party review.

Combined MASW + Refraction Package

A single crew deploys both arrays in one mobilisation. The refraction tomogram gives you the P-wave velocity and the depth to engineering rock; the MASW line gives you the stiffness. Together they constrain Poisson’s ratio and reduce uncertainty in the deformation modulus for settlement analysis.

Quick answers

How much does a MASW survey cost on a typical Bedford site?

For a single 46-metre array with full dispersion analysis, inversion, and a signed report, budget between £1,190 and £2,420. The spread depends on how many lines you need, whether we combine it with seismic refraction, and the access conditions on site. A two-line setup on a clear plot north of the river will sit at the lower end; a multi-line survey on a congested town-centre site with traffic management pushes toward the upper figure.

What depth does the MASW method reach in the Oxford Clay?

With a 46-metre active spread and a sledgehammer source, we reliably resolve shear-wave velocity to about 30–35 metres below ground level. In the stiff Oxford Clay found across much of Bedford, the Rayleigh-wave penetration is good and the fundamental-mode dispersion curve is well-defined. If the site has a thick granular overburden—river terrace gravels, for example—higher-mode contamination can appear, and we extend the record length to separate the modes clearly.

Can you run the line on tarmac or concrete?

Yes, we plant the geophones on paved surfaces using a thin gypsum or bentonite couplant that ensures mechanical contact without damaging the geophone spike. The sledgehammer source still generates usable Rayleigh-wave energy on hard standing, though we switch to a heavier hammer and a steel striker plate if the surface is reinforced concrete. The coupling check on every channel confirms data quality before we start the stack.

What do I actually get in the report?

A BS 5930-compliant factual report containing the processed dispersion image, the picked fundamental-mode curve, the inverted 1D shear-wave velocity profile, and the calculated VS30 with the corresponding Eurocode 8 ground type.
